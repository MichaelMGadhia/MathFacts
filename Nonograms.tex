\section{What Are Nonograms?}

Nonogram puzzles, also called Picross, are puzzles solved by completing a grid of squares.
Using numbers for each row and column, you must determine whether each square is full (which we'll fill in black) or empty (which we'll mark with $\times$).
Often, the end result will be a picture of something!

In each row or column, the numbers (in order) tell you about the filled cells in that row.
For example, a 2 means there's a block of 2 filled cells touching each other in that line.
A 3 and then a 1 means there is a block of 3 filled cells, then some amount of empty cells to separate the blocks, then 1 filled cell on its own.
Let's look at some visual examples.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{1,3}}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

There's only one way to fill this row.
The first three cells are the block of 3, then there's room for only one empty space, then the last cell must be the 1.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{1,3}}
        \nonogramrow{1}{1/3, 5/1}
        \placex{4}{1}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Sometimes, we can't immediately solve a line.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Here, there are three possibilities.
If the block of 5 starts on the first cell, there could be one or two spaces after it, so the block of 3 could start on the seventh or eighth cell.
Otherwise, the 5 can start on the second cell, leaving just enough room for the 3 to start on the eighth.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramfill{10}{1}
        \nonogramrow{1}{1/5, 7/3}
    \end{nonogram}

    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramfill{10}{1}
        \nonogramrow{1}{1/5, 8/3}
    \end{nonogram}

    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramfill{10}{1}
        \nonogramrow{1}{2/5, 8/3}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

But that doesn't mean we can't make progress!
Notice how in all possible ways this could be solved, some cells are always filled by the 5 and the 3.
This means we can definitely fill in those cells.
If this is a row in a larger puzzle, each of those cells also belongs to a column with numbers, so doing that might lead to progress on a column.

\begin{center}    
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramrow{1}{2/4, 8/2}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Here is a nonogram puzzle with 5 rows and 5 columns, and its solution on the right (it's supposed to be a picture of a dog).
Try to work through it yourself, starting with the second column that has a 4.
Using what you know already, you might be able to recreate the solution on the right using logic, without copying it.
Don't worry if that's still confusing, we'll solve one step by step in the next section and learn some useful techniques.
