    \foreach \i in {1,...,#1}

    }

\section{What Are Nonograms?}

Nonogram puzzles, also called Picross, are puzzles solved by completing a grid of squares.
Using numbers for each row and column, you must determine whether each square is full (which we'll fill in black) or empty (which we'll mark with $\times$).
Often, the end result will be a picture of something!

In each row or column, the numbers (in order) tell you about the filled cells in that row.
For example, a 2 means there's a block of 2 filled cells touching each other in that line.
A 3 and then a 1 means there is a block of 3 filled cells, then some amount of empty cells to separate the blocks, then 1 filled cell on its own.
Let's look at some visual examples.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{1,3}}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

There's only one way to fill this row.
The first three cells are the block of 3, then there's room for only one empty space, then the last cell must be the 1.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{1,3}}
        \nonogramrow{1}{1/3, 5/1}
        \placex{4}{1}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Sometimes, we can't immediately solve a line.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Here, there are three possibilities.
If the block of 5 starts on the first cell, there could be one or two spaces after it, so the block of 3 could start on the seventh or eighth cell.
Otherwise, the 5 can start on the second cell, leaving just enough room for the 3 to start on the eighth.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramfill{10}{1}
        \nonogramrow{1}{1/5, 7/3}
    \end{nonogram}

    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramfill{10}{1}
        \nonogramrow{1}{1/5, 8/3}
    \end{nonogram}

    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramfill{10}{1}
        \nonogramrow{1}{2/5, 8/3}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

But that doesn't mean we can't make progress!
Notice how in all possible ways this could be solved, some cells are always filled by the 5 and the 3.
This means we can definitely fill in those cells.
If this is a row in a larger puzzle, each of those cells also belongs to a column with numbers, so doing that might lead to progress on a column.

\begin{center}    
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=1,columns=10,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2, solution=true]
        \nonogramV{{3,5}}
        \nonogramrow{1}{2/4, 8/2}
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Here is a nonogram puzzle with 5 rows and 5 columns.
Try to work through it yourself.
Using what you know already, you might be able to get the solution, or the rules might still be confusing.
Don't worry, we'll solve it step by step in the next section.

\begin{center}
    % Example puzzle
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]
        % Pay attention to order numbers are defined in, since I don't fully understand it without example

        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

% Worked example with step-by-step visuals
\subsection{Solving a Nonogram}

Let's start by looking at columns, and look at rows after we do what we can.
The columns with 1s in them don't tell us much yet, but the column with a 4 can only be one of two things (the 4 starts at the top or the bottom), and in both cases the middle three cells are filled.
We'll mark those for now.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]
        
        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}
        \nonogramcolumn{2}{2/3}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Then, the last column can only be in one of three positions, and all of them have the middle cell filled.
Take a moment to make sure that makes sense based on what we've done so far.
We'll mark that too.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]

        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}
        \nonogramcolumn{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramcolumn{5}{3/1}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Now, let's look at the rows.
The second row is already solved, so we'll mark all empty squares.
In the third row, the cell on the far right is filled, and that must be the 1, so the cell to its left can't also be filled.
We'll mark empty squares for both of those rows.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]
        
        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}
        \nonogramcolumn{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramcolumn{5}{3/1}
        \placex{4}{3}
        \placex{1}{4}
        \placex{3}{4}
        \placex{4}{4}
        \placex{5}{4}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Also, the row with a 4 tells us the middle 3 cells in it are filled, just like the column.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]
       
        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}
        \nonogramrow{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramcolumn{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramcolumn{5}{3/1}
        
        \placex{4}{3}
        \placex{1}{4}
        \placex{3}{4}
        \placex{4}{4}
        \placex{5}{4}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

Back to the columns, the last column has only one possibility, where the 3 is on the bottom.
We'll fill in that block and mark the top cell empty.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]
        
        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}
        \nonogramcolumn{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramcolumn{5}{1/3}
        \nonogramrow{2}{2/3}
        \placex{4}{3}
        \placex{1}{4}
        \placex{3}{4}
        \placex{4}{4}
        \placex{5}{4}
        \placex{5}{5}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

There are easier ways to progress now, but notice that the middle column has only one solution.
Since it only contains 1s, its bottom and middle cell must be empty to avoid making a block of 2.
That means its filled cell in the fourth row is the bottom of the two 1s, and the other one has one space left at the top.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]

        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}
        \nonogramcolumn{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramcolumn{5}{1/3}
        \nonogramrow{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramrow{5}{3/1}
        \placex{4}{3}
        \placex{1}{4}
        \placex{3}{4}
        \placex{4}{4}
        \placex{5}{4}
        \placex{5}{5}
        \placex{3}{1}
        \placex{3}{3}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

We're almost done.
The first and fourth rows are done as is the fourth column, so we can finish them with empty cells.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]

        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}
        \nonogramcolumn{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramcolumn{5}{1/3}
        \nonogramrow{2}{2/3}
        \nonogramrow{5}{3/1}
        \placex{4}{3}
        \placex{1}{4}
        \placex{3}{4}
        \placex{4}{4}
        \placex{5}{4}
        \placex{5}{5}
        \placex{3}{1}
        \placex{3}{3}
        \placex{4}{1}
        \placex{4}{5}
        \placex{1}{5}
        \placex{2}{5}
        \placex{1}{2}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

It should be easy now to finish the puzzle!

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=5,columns=5,scale=0.35,width=3cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=2]

        \nonogramV{{1,1},{4},{1,2},{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{4},{1,1},{1},{3}}

        % nonogramfill command defined above
        \nonogramfill{5}{5}

        \nonogramrow{5}{3/1}
        \nonogramrow{4}{2/1}
        \nonogramrow{3}{1/2, 5/1}
        \nonogramrow{2}{2/4}
        \nonogramrow{1}{2/1, 5/1}
        
    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

It's a (very simple) picture of a dog!

That's all I plan to include on simple solving techniques.
The next sections assume you have a basic understanding of what a nonogram puzzle is, and could solve one like the example above.

\section{Solvability}

You might want to try creating a picture of squares, and then writing numbers around it to make a nonogram.
If you have done that and give it to someone to solve (or solve it yourself without using the answer), you might find that you can't solve it with what you know.
There are a couple reasons this happens, which turn out to be interesting properties of nonograms.

\subsection{Number of Solutions}
Not every nonogram has one solution!

We'll make very small examples to demonstrate this.
This nonogram has a single unique solution.
Given the numbers in the puzzle, you can get to the exact picture on the right without guessing.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=2,columns=2,scale=0.35,width=2cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=1]

        \nonogramV{{1},{2}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{2}}

    \end{nonogram}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=2,columns=2,scale=0.35,width=2cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=1]

        \nonogramV{{1},{2}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{2}}
        \nonogramrow{2}{1/2}
        \nonogramrow{1}{2/1}
        \placex{1}{1}
        \puzzlestrut

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

This nonogram has no solution.
The first row would fill in both top cells, but then both the columns are solved, and there's no way for the bottom row to be solved.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=2,columns=2,scale=0.35,width=2cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=1]

        \nonogramV{{1},{2}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{1}}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

This nonogram has two possible solutions!
The two solutions pictured both satisfy all requirements from rows and columns, so there isn't one unique way to fill in the cells.

\begin{center}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=2,columns=2,scale=0.35,width=2cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=1]

        \nonogramV{{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{1}}

    \end{nonogram}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=2,columns=2,scale=0.35,width=2cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=1]

        \nonogramV{{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{1}}
        \nonogramrow{1}{1/1}
        \nonogramrow{2}{2/1}
        \nonogramfill{2}{2}

    \end{nonogram}
    \begin{nonogram}[rows=2,columns=2,scale=0.35,width=2cm,
        fontsize=footnotesize,helplines=5,extracells=1]

        \nonogramV{{1},{1}}
        \nonogramH{{1},{1}}
        \nonogramrow{1}{2/1}
        \nonogramrow{2}{1/1}
        \nonogramfill{2}{2}

    \end{nonogram}
\end{center}

This means not all nonograms correspond to a single picture.
But, most puzzles made for humans to solve are designed to have a unique solution.
